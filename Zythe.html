History

On a different world, a village of humans lived. They were much similar to us. They never truly knew where they came from, just like us, and they could only imagine that another world existed with other humans on it.

They lived in a cold, coastal tundra with very little plant and animal life. There were a few other villages nearby that kept in contact with this one, but never very regularly, as the terrain and climate made the trip very dangerous. The village of around 300 people relied on a very large ocean dwelling animal, similar to whales. They made water and windproof coats, sheets, and membranes out of it's skin, and structures, such as houses, out of its bones. They ate the animal's flesh for food, and used it's fat for candles and fire. They made string out of strong, tall, fibrous plants that grew in the water.

The village needed these resources, so every fortnight a large fishing boat with about 20 people was sent out to catch one of these creatures. It was a big risk for the village to send so many people on one trip, but the trips had a very low failure rate, and if they didn't make the trips, they would die. The boats were designed to be very stable, made of the bones, fiber, skins, and driftwood, weather was forecasted, waves were measured, and in the rare event of a boat sinking, they had smaller lifeboats to save some of their lives.

On one such trip, exactly what they feared happened. An unusually strong storm struck their homelands. They rode waves taller than their houses, winds stronger than the mostly inexperienced crew and their more experienced mentors had ever seen. A harpoon poked through the strong hide of the boat, and the boat started taking on water. Rain starts pouring, causing visibility to drop.

In their panic, 12 people went into lifeboats and flee. The other 9 started trying to repair the larger boat. The hole grew bigger and bigger, and at some point all of the people on board were taking apart the boat frantically, trying to salvage any floatable driftwood they can. One last wave rips the boat in half and 7 people are never seen again.

After the rain and winds clear, it became very clear that the shore is gone. The people still alive were out on the open ocean, and the wind had pushed them north. One boat had 9 people on it, the other one had 3. In the morning, the larger boat found a stranded woman, clinging onto a piece of driftwood. They welcomed her onboard. The two boats eventually found each other and the passengers connected the two boats together. Later that day, they came across a dead body floating on a piece of driftwood.

Winds and water currents pushed the boat north, and away from their homeland. They were running out of food and water and were planning on what to do. The next day, something incredible happened. They discovered an island in the distance. They used their training and strength to bring themselves towards it, but when they arrived, they realized it wasn't an island, it was a plant!

In more tropical areas, a plant has found a way to take advantage of the ocean and its sunlight. It grows on the water, growing strong air sacs that it floats on. A huge leaf is connected via a strong 4m high stalk to the air sacs. The leaf expands outwards from the center and grows other stalks downwards, which, when they hit water, create more air sacs. A whole ecosystem lives on the island. Animals, free from predators, eat small ocean creatures, and when they die, the plant decomposes their body and gains the nutrients that were in the oceans. In return, the island uses reverse osmosis and collects rainwater from the many storms to create fresh water for the animals. Mosses and fungi grow on the floor of the island, consuming energy that the main plant missed. When they died, they were consumed by the plant. Some of these islands can grow to be over a half kilometer wide.

Our travelers found an island that measured around 300m. It normally inhabited much warmer waters, but was blown further south than normal by the storm. They were relieved to find it. It had fresh water, edible plants, and a few species of small fish eating animals. For now, they were safe. They continued to drift northwards towards the equator, pushed by winds and ocean currents. After 5 months of drifting, the island approached the shores of a new continent.

This new continent was a jungle, uninhabited by humans before. There was an excess of plants, foods, herbs, fungi, animals, sunlight, and warmth. Rain fell every day and caused low areas to flood. In this new land, these people made the best of their new environment. They stopped wearing their fat filled whale-skin clothing, and started to eat what they had around them. These 13 people grew a brand new civilization rapidly, and they prospered for a very long time. These are the Zythë, and this is their language.

Phonotactics

Syllables are made by combining a consonant with a vowel, consonant being first. For example: /si/ is a syllable that can exist in Zythë, but /s/ or /i/ may not. Words are made out of 1 or more syllables stuck together, for example, /si.βe/ is a possible word. However, there is an exception to this rule: a vowel may be added at the beginning of a word, and/or a consonant may be added at the end of a word. For example: /ꭓuzi/ is a possible word, but so is /eꭓuzi/, /ꭓuzik/, and /eꭓuzik/

If two vowels touch in a word for grammatical reasons, then only one of them can exist. Refer to the vowel hierarchy below. The vowel that is lower down in the hierarchy will disappear. If the two vowels are in the same place in the hierarchy, different things will happen depending on what is grammatically more important for the sentence.

The letter < i > is pronounced as either /i/ or /ɪ/ depending on the dialect and context.

All letters are always lowercase, even at the beginning of sentences. Periods are used at the end of sentences.

Nouns

Declension

Nouns in Zythë are declined for case and number. Different nouns have different endings and decline in different ways. To decline a noun, remove the last vowel and add the ending shown by the correct chart. If a noun ends in < y > (except the pronouns < Qy, Py >), then it will decline as shown by the 2nd table, labeled "Y Form". All other nouns are declined as shown by the first table, labeled "Standard Form"

If an ending has an asterisk attacked, then the first letter of the root word needs to be devoiced, if it is a consonant. If the first letter is < k >, then it gets transformed into < q >

If two vowels in the same place in the hierarchy touch due to declension, the vowel in the ending will take precedent.

Zythë is a Fluid-S Active-Stative language, which means that sometimes the subject of an intransitive verb will be marked with the agentive case, and sometimes with the patientive case. It will get marked agentive if the action was deliberate, and patientive if the action was involuntary.

Pronouns

There are eight pronouns, one singular and one plural for each of the four persons. The 1st and 2nd persons are the normal 1st and 2nd persons, but the 3rd and 4th persons are the proximate and obviative third persons respectively. The two versions distinguish between two different third persons in a conversation. The proximate, or 3rd person as we are calling it here, is the most important in the conversation, generally the first pronoun to get used. The obviative pronoun is the less prominant one, generally the second one to be used.

Honorifics

There are four classes of honor that can be placed on someone. They are:

  1. Respect - This is almost always used when talking to people who you would consider dating OR people at least about 20 years older than you. This class is used to show respect to someone you may or may not know. It can also be used for strangers and people you want to be particularly nice to.
  2. Demonstrative - For people at least a few years younger than you. It can also be used is someone is being childish or immature, or if you want to mock that person for being childish or immature. It's often times used among a group of male friends who like to poke/make fun of each other.
  3. Friends/Family - For people you are close friends with, or close family members; people you would be devastated to lose.
  4. Extreme Respect/Fame - This is almost always used when talking to people you have extreme respect for, usually celebrities and elders. It can also be a more exaggerated form of H1

They can be attached to most nouns relating to people by using the following suffixes:

  1. r(e)-
  2. i(q)-
  3. ve(s)-
  4. dhe(p)-

Honorifics have become so ingrained with greetings that some words are completely different depending on honorifics.

The word for "hello" is normally esiq, but is different for the following honorifics:

  1. req
  2. iqî
  3. vesoq
  4. dhepiq

Verbs

Evidentiality, Transitivity, & Negativity

A prefix is added before all verbs that denotes Evidentiality, Transitivity, and Negativity.

V. stands for First-Hand/Sensory, which means the speaker personally experienced the action, maybe by seeing it happen, hearing it happen, or any other sense, or because it happened entirely inside their head
I. stands for Inferential, which means the speaker inferred the action. Maybe Jeff is sad, and the speaker inferred that he was sad due to the fact that he was crying.
R. stands for Reportative, which means that the speaker is reporting what someone else told them.

If two vowels come into contact this way, < t > goes in between the two vowels

For Example:

vage (v.) - swim
ebhi (v.) - work
sypo (v.) - fight
go (v.) - sleep

qo y-vage
1SG.A INTR-swim
I swam (today)

qo yt-ebhi
1SG.A INTR-work
I worked (today)

shïto qy sypo
1SG.AGT 1SG.PAT fight
He/she fights me

shïto tunoko ø-sypo
3SG.AGT 4SG.PAT TRANS.REP-fight
He/she fought him/her

shïtë ana-go
3SG.P INTR.INFR.NEG-sleep
He/she doesn't look like they slept (last night)

Tense, Modality, Aspect

Verbs are conjugated based on Tense, Modality, and Aspect. Right now I don't have Modality done, but it will be a prefix to the verb when I finish it. For now, verbs are conjugated with Tense and Aspect.

The chart below shows the prefixes that correspond to the different verb conjugations

Perfect Continuous Habitual
Future -eph -ifi
Present (no ending) -q -xï
Recent Past (no ending)* -la* -las*
Distant Past -kä* -të*
Ancient Past -me shï* -køth*

*for all of the past tenses, sometimes the last vowel of the root verb will be modified. Only five vowels get modified, and how they are modified is shown below:

e → ø
i → y
a → ä
ø → o
y → u

If the vowel isn't in the first column on the chart, then it doesn't get converted,and it just stays normal.

The different past tenses don't have exact definitions, but there is a general guide on how to use them. Recent Past is for when an action happened very recently, usually at most about 3 weeks in the past. Distant past is for when an action happened within the lifespan of everyone on the planet, which right now is about 110-120 years old. Anything of the ancient past is anything that's older than that, such as the US Civil War, the US Revolutionary War, the Roman Empire, etc.

Verbs will always end in a vowel, so there should be no phonotactic conflicts.

Because both the present and past have no ending, and the vowel in the root only sometimes changes, sometimes the recent past and present can merge and become indistinguishable.
