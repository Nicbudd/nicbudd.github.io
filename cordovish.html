§1.1 The Cordovish Vowel System

Cordovish has six vowels arranged in a triangle form: two front vowels in /e/ and /i/, two central vowels in /a/ and /ɨ/, and two back vowels in /o/ and /u/. Vowels in Cordovish can be either long or short, with the exception of /ɨ/ which only occurs as a short vowel, meaning that Cordovish has a total of eleven vowel sounds. Short vowels are written in Cordovish orthography as < a e i í o u >, while long vowels are written as < ā ē ī ō ū >, where the macron diacritic marks a lengthened vowel. This table summarizes the orthography and the phonetic value of each vowel.

Vowel Short Long

i /i/ < i > /iː/ < ī >

§1.1.1 Short Vowels

The six short short vowels of Cordovish are written in Cordovish orthography as < a e i í o u >. Short vowels are significantly more common in Cordovish than long vowels. Cordovish short vowels generally do not occur next to one another unless there is a syllable break between them or they form a diphthong. An example of both of these is the word ‘siau’ (shah), which contains a syllable break and a diphthong that renders its pronunciation [si.au]. Some exceptions do exist however, such as the word ‘vie’ (bee) where the short vowels < ie > form the short vowel /i/. These exceptions are few and far between, however, and it can thus be fairly safely assumed that any meeting of two short vowels indicates either a diphthong or a syllable break.

Examples of short vowels:
sannt [sanːt] ‘saint’ vílkín/típpír [vɨlkɨn ; tɨpːɨr] ‘tiger’
vell [vel] ‘weld’ kobolt [kobolt] ‘kobold’
ind [ind] ‘in, within’ śurun [ʃurun] ‘jury’

§1.1.2 Long Vowels

The five long vowels of Cordovish are written in Cordovish orthography as < ā ē ī ō ū >. As mentioned previously, the vowel /ɨ/ only occurs in short form, and therefore has no long variant. Long vowels in Cordovish are not especially common, making up perhaps twenty percent of all vowels. Long vowels never occur next to each other, with the exception of loan words and some transliterations. Only the vowels < ā ū > can occur in the initial position, while < ā ē ī ō ū > can occur in medial and final positions.

Examples of long vowels:
fādźirín [faːdʒirɨn] ‘big cat’ nōdil [noːdil] ‘whisker’
bedē [bedeː] ‘crow’ vūf [vuːf] ‘woof; bark’
krīsa [kriːsa] ‘raccoon’

§1.1.3 Short and Long Vowels

Short and long vowels in Cordovish generally do not go together, as is true of vowels in Cordovish in general. Sometimes, however—typically when a suffix or prefix is appended to a word, or when words are combined—short and long vowels can exist beside each other. This is seen in constructions like ‘bedēan’ (crows), ‘sāmīan’ (tanukis), and others. In circumstances like this, a syllable break falls between the two vowels and separates them—thus, ‘bedēan’ is [be.deː.an] and ‘sāmīan’ is [saː.miː.an]. This is also true of diphthongs. ‘Auind’ (within) and ‘lēeum’ (tentacle) are respectively pronounced [au.ind] and [leː.eum].

§1.1.4 Diphthongs

Cordovish has 8 diphthongs: /aɪ au eɪ eu ui ue ia ua/, which are represented orthographically as < ai au ei eu ui ue ia ua >. Diphthongs in Cordovish can only occur between two short vowels, and thus there are no long vowels within these diphthongs. The phoneme /ɪ/, which is represented in two of these diphthongs (/aɪ eɪ/) occurs only as an allophone of /i/ in those diphthongs, although it can be an allophone of /i/ in any situation for some speakers of Cordovish. Some Cordovish diphthongs, under certain circumstances, can be contracted or otherwise modified by letters that surround them, as is detailed in §1.1.4.1 Diphthong Modification and Contraction.

beflai [beflaɪ] ‘butterfly’ teñsuivara [teɲsuivara] ‘dinosaur’
saunur [saunur] ‘say’ bueź [bueʒ] ‘rich person’
leittivun [leɪtːivun] ‘oak wood’ stizia [stizia] ‘antler’
leumi [leumi] ‘be disliked’ drakuari [drakwari] ‘draguar’
§1.1.4.1 Diphthong Modification and /w/-allophony
§1.1.4.1.1 Modification

Some diphthongs are modified when they occur around certain vowel sounds, specifically /e/ and /u/. These diphthong changes occur only when words are combined, or a suffix, prefix, or infix is added to a word. The diphthongs which are affected by this are /aɪ eɪ ue ia/ < ai ei ue ia >. These four diphthongs only in small, fairly limited circumstances, and the rules for change can be summarized as follows:

  • < ai > changes to < ei > when the letter < e > follows it;
  • < ei > changes to < eu > when the letter < a > follows it;
  • < eu > changes to < ei > when the letter < i > follows it;
  • and < ia > changes to < iu > when the letter < u > follows it.

The results of these rules can be seen here:

Word Combination Result
beflai ‘butterfly’ → beflai-elun → befleilun ‘caterpillar’
alei ‘bell’ → alei-auvuś → aleuś ‘cracked bell’
liteu ‘stem’ → liteu-itu → liteitu ‘leaf stem’
stizia ‘antler’ → stizia-uri → stiziuri ‘deer antler’

Other examples include ‘vemiuri’ (deer call); ‘teitu’ (tea leaf); etc.

§1.1.4.1.2 /w/-allophony

/w/ occurs as an allophone to /u/ with two Cordovish diphthongs, these being < ua ue > /ua ue/. In medial position following the plosives /t d k ɡ/, the /u/ in these diphthongs becomes the sound /w/; this means that ‘drakuari’ (draguar) is affected and is pronounced [drakwari] rather than [*drakuari], but ‘bueź’ (rich person) is not affected and remains [bueʒ] rather than becoming [*bweʒ]. Because it is specific only to these two diphthongs and occurs only following the plosives /t d k ɡ/, /w/-allophony is not an especially prominent feature of Cordovish. Only a few common words like ‘drakuari’ are affected by this sound change.

§1.1.5 Semivowels

Cordovish has one true semivowel in /j/, which is orthographically represented as < j >. /j/ is mostly a consonant, and is treated as one in most positions; while it can sometimes act as a vowel, it predominantly does so in the initial position. In medial and final positions, it is virtually always a consonant. /j/ does not have a long variant and cannot occur as long in any position. /w/, the other traditional semivowel, does not occur naturally in Cordovish except as an allophone of /u/; as such, it is not a semivowel and cannot be treated as one.

§1.2 Stress

Stress in Cordovish is truly unpredictable, but there are some patterns which can generally be found in how stress occurs in Cordovish.

In most words, primary stress will fall on either the first or second syllable of a word, unless that word includes the affricates /ts tʃ dʒ/ or one of the fricatives /s z ʃ ʒ/, in which case the stress will tend to fall either on the syllable immediately preceding these sounds or the syllable with them. Compare, for example, the word “ravuñ” (desk) to “ravuntsí” (city desk). In the former, stress falls [ˈravuɲ]; in the latter, [raˈvuntsi]. Another example of this is “julung” (college) and “juluntsí” (college town), stressed [ˈjuluŋ] and [juˈluŋtsi] respectively. However, this is not hard and fast: “iśdau” [ˈiʃdau̯] and its verb form “iśdaūr” [iʃˈdauːr] are contrary examples to this rule.

Additionally, some words do not follow the trend of primary stress on the first or second syllable; these words typically being words with more than three syllables. In the word “teñsuivara” (dinosaur) for example, the stress falls on the third syllable such that it is pronounced [teɲsuiˈvara] rather than one of [*ˈteɲsuivara] and [*teɲˈsuivara].

§1.3.2 Summary of sound and orthography changes

The following is a comprehensive list of all sound and orthography changes that take place in Cordovish. Cordovish orthography generally reflects the sound changes that occur—there are only a few exceptions to this rule, and for the most part such exceptions are limited to a few words. Sounds which do not appear on this list do not undergo any changes in sound or in the orthography.

/m/ and /n/

The sound /m/ can occur in short form /m/ < m > or long form /mː/. Depending on what comes before or after it, however, the form /mː/ can be represented in two ways orthographically, these being < mm mn >.

eum [eum] ‘leg’
khem [çem] ‘pen’
leumi [leumi] ‘be disliked’
lēeummik [leːeumːik] ‘tentacle-like’
khemmik [çemːik] ‘pen-like’
kadiummik [kadiumːik] ‘sodium-like’

When /n/ is followed by the sound /m/, the resulting cluster /nm/ < nm > changes to /mː/ and becomes the cluster < mm >.

omme [omːe] ‘of the’ ( < on·me)
umme [umːe] ‘on the’ ( < un·me)
immet [imːet] ‘in the’ ( < in·met)

When /m/ is followed by /n/, the resulting cluster is /mː/ but this is written orthographically < mn >.

dīmne [diːmːe] 'plain'
timno [timːo] 'flower'
annumne [anːumːe] 'aluminum'

This is also true when /m/ is followed by /ɲ/. The cluster that results is /mː/, written as < mn >; however, this combination is considerably more infrequent than /m/ + /n/ and typically only occurs in naturalized words that already have an underlying /mn/ or /mɲ/ within them. Thus the few words in which this occurs are words such as ‘amnesia’ or ‘amnesty’.

amnīsia [amːiːsia] 'amnesia'
amnide [amːide] 'amnesty'

The sound /n/ can occur in short form as < n >, and this is the primary form in which it occurs in Cordovish. However, /n/ also has a long form in /nː/, which is created when /n/ follows another /n/. This cluster is written as < nn >.

tennu [tenːu] 'believe'
eunne [eunːe] 'he/she/it is'
sannt [sanːt] 'saint'

Some speakers may—in certain circumstances—break this cluster into two syllables such that tennu and eunne are pronounced as [ten.nu] and [eun.ne] respectively; this is generally considered to be a nonstandard dialectal pronunciation. However, in these words there is an underlying second /n/ sound such that most speakers will pronounce the words as [tenː.nu] and [eunː.ne] respectively. Either this or the official [tenːu] and [eunːe] are considered acceptable, standard pronunciations.

/ɲ/

The sound /ɲ/ occurs only in short form, and both modifies and can be modified by sounds that follow it. /ɲ/ modifies in the cluster /ɲç/, which is reduced to /ɲk/ < ñk >. When followed by the sounds /ts tʃ dʒ ʃ ʒ/, /ɲ/ is modified to /ni/ < ni >.

§1.2 Stress

Stress in Cordovish is truly unpredictable, but there are some patterns which can generally be found in how stress occurs in Cordovish.

In most words, primary stress will fall on either the first or second syllable of a word, unless that word includes the affricates /ts tʃ dʒ/ or one of the fricatives /s z ʃ ʒ/, in which case the stress will tend to fall either on the syllable immediately preceding these sounds or the syllable with them. Compare, for example, the word “ravuñ” (desk) to “ravuntsí” (city desk). In the former, stress falls [ˈravuɲ]; in the latter, [raˈvuntsi]. Another example of this is “julung” (college) and “juluntsí” (college town), stressed [ˈjuluŋ] and [juˈluŋtsi] respectively. However, this is not hard and fast: “iśdau” [ˈiʃdau̯] and its verb form “iśdaūr” [iʃˈdauːr] are contrary examples to this rule.

Additionally, some words do not follow the trend of primary stress on the first or second syllable; these words typically being words with more than three syllables. §2 Nouns

Cordovish nouns are declined according to case and number. There are four cases (ablative, dative, genitive, and locative) and two numbers (singular and plural); in practice, however, declension is much more simple than this description would make it seem. The singular and plural forms of the genitive and the ablative cases have merged, meaning that plural forms of the dative and locative cases are the only cases which must be declined appropriately.

§3 Articles

§3.1 Definite and indefinite articles

Cordovish has three articles—two definite and one indefinite. The definite article which corresponds to English “the” has a singular and plural form—these are < me; met > respectively. The indefinite which corresponds to English “an” has a single form used for both, which is < men >.

Article Singular Plural
Denifite (the) me met
Indefinite men

Examples of Cordovish articles:

me alei ‘the bell’; me vie ‘the bee’; me lē ‘the sea’; me teñsuivara ‘the dinosaur’;

met aleun ‘the bells’; met viean ‘the bees’; met lēan ‘the seas’; met teñsuivarān ‘the dinosaurs’

men alei ‘a bell’; men vie ‘a bee’; men lē ‘a sea’; men teñsuivara ‘a dinosaur’

§3.1.1 Contradiction of the definite article

The prepositions di (to), don (of), tun (on), and ind (in) combine with the definite article to form single word contractions.

Preposition Reduced form Singular Plural
di i ime imet
don on omme ommet
tun un umme ummet
ind in imme immet

The reduced forms of di, don, tun, and ind are, respectively, i, on, un, and in; the singular forms are ime, omme, umme, and imme; and the plural forms arei, on, un, and in; the singular forms are imet, ommet, ummet, and immet. In the singular forms the cluster < nm > becomes < mm >.
